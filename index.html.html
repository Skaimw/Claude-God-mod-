Chapter 01Why Most People Get Mediocre Results

Most people treat Claude like a fancier Google search. They type a fragment — "explain marketing" or "write an email" — hit enter, and then wonder why the response feels generic. The truth is, the quality of what you get back is almost entirely determined by the quality of what you put in.

Claude is not a vending machine. It's more like a brilliant, well-read colleague who happens to have expertise in almost every field imaginable. You wouldn't walk up to that colleague and say "give me something useful." You'd have a conversation, provide context, and ask a specific question.

"The prompt is not the command. The prompt is the beginning of a collaboration."

The Core Principle of Prompt Engineering

The Three Common Mistakes

MISTAKE 01
Being Too Vague
Asking "write me a business plan" gives you a template. Asking for a plan for a specific business, audience, and goal gives you something usable.
MISTAKE 02
No Context Given
Claude doesn't know who you are, what you know, or what you're trying to accomplish unless you say so. Context is everything.
MISTAKE 03
Accepting Draft One
The first response is a starting point, not the final answer. The best results come from iteration and feedback, not single-shot prompts.
THE FIX
Treat It Like a Briefing
Before you type, ask yourself: who is Claude playing, what do I need, who is this for, and in what format? Answer those four questions in your prompt.
The Mindset Shift

Stop asking "what can Claude do?" and start asking "what do I need, and how do I describe that precisely?" The ceiling on Claude's output is almost always set by the ceiling on the clarity of your request.

Chapter 02The Anatomy of a Perfect Prompt

Every great prompt has the same skeleton. Once you know it, you'll never write a weak prompt again. Think of it as a four-part briefing you give before asking Claude to do any significant task.

01
Role — Who Is Claude?

Tell Claude what kind of expert, persona, or perspective to adopt. "You are a senior copywriter at a direct-response agency" will produce very different output than "write me an ad."

02
Task — What Exactly Is Needed?

Be precise. Use action verbs: analyze, rewrite, compare, outline, critique, summarize. Avoid vague nouns like "something about" or "some content."

03
Context — What Does Claude Need to Know?

Audience, purpose, constraints, background. Paste in relevant documents. Mention what you've already tried. Specify what to avoid.

04
Format — How Should the Answer Look?

Length, structure, tone, file format, language level. "Respond in three short paragraphs with no bullet points" is a perfectly valid instruction.

Before vs. After

❌ Weak Prompt

Write a LinkedIn post about AI.

✓ Strong Prompt

You are a B2B SaaS marketer. Write a 150-word LinkedIn post for a founder audience about how AI is changing customer support — conversational tone, end with a thought-provoking question, no hashtags.

Template to copy:

You are [role/expert]. Your task is to [specific action].

Context: [background, audience, constraints, goal]

Format: [length, structure, tone, style]

Chapter 03
Persona Power
Assign Claude a role and watch it lean into expertise you'd otherwise never access
Chapter 03Persona Power

One of the most underused techniques in prompting is the role assignment. By telling Claude who it is before asking a question, you fundamentally shift the frame through which it approaches the answer. The same question gets a different — and often far better — response when asked to an expert than to a generic assistant.

This works because the role sets a context for vocabulary, priorities, assumptions, and depth. A lawyer and a startup founder approach the same contract question very differently. Both perspectives have value — but you need to choose which one serves your purpose.

Powerful Persona Patterns

PATTERN 01
The Domain Expert
"You are a Harvard-trained behavioral economist." Unlocks technical depth, field-specific frameworks, and professional vocabulary.
PATTERN 02
The Devil's Advocate
"You are a skeptical investor who will challenge every assumption." Surfaces weaknesses in plans, arguments, and ideas you'd miss otherwise.
PATTERN 03
The Audience Proxy
"You are my ideal customer: a 35-year-old small business owner who is skeptical of new tech." Gives feedback as if from the real person you're trying to reach.
PATTERN 04
The Specific Voice
"You are a first-year medical student who explains things simply." Controls complexity and vocabulary to match any audience level.

You are a senior UX researcher with 10 years of experience in fintech. I'm going to share wireframes for a new onboarding flow. Your job is to identify friction points a first-time user would encounter, ranked by severity. Be direct — don't soften feedback.

[paste wireframe description here]

Pro Tip

Stack personas for richer output: "You are a former McKinsey consultant who now runs a bootstrapped startup." Dual backgrounds create nuanced perspectives that generic prompts never produce.

Chapter 04
Chain of Thought
Force Claude to reason step-by-step before giving you the answer — and watch accuracy soar
Chapter 04Chain of Thought

For complex problems, the single biggest improvement you can make to any prompt is four words: "think step by step." This technique — called Chain of Thought prompting — was discovered by AI researchers and remains one of the most reliably powerful tools in the prompt engineer's toolkit.

When Claude reasons out loud before giving an answer, it's more accurate, more thorough, and more likely to catch its own errors. You're essentially asking for the scratchpad, not just the final answer. For analysis, math, planning, and decisions, this is non-negotiable.

"Asking for the answer is easy. Asking for the reasoning behind the answer is where the real value hides."

Chain of Thought Principle

Trigger Phrases That Activate Deeper Thinking

Phrase When to Use It
"Think step by step before answering." Any complex analysis or decision
"Show your reasoning, then give your conclusion." Math, logic, argument evaluation
"Before you answer, list any assumptions you're making." Strategic planning, advice
"What are the strongest counterarguments to your answer?" Any one-sided analysis
"Critique your own response after you write it." When quality and accuracy matter most

Think step by step.

I'm deciding whether to raise prices by 15% or launch a new lower-tier product. My business has 800 monthly subscribers, 12% monthly churn, and average LTV of $340. Before giving a recommendation, list your assumptions, work through the revenue impact of each option, identify the key risks, and then give me your ranked recommendation with reasoning.

Few-Shot & Examples
The fastest way to teach Claude your exact standard — without a single explanation
Chapter 05Few-Shot & Examples

Describing what you want is good. Showing what you want is better. Few-shot prompting means including one to three examples of the kind of output you're looking for — and letting Claude pattern-match to them. The quality jump is often dramatic.

This technique is especially powerful for creative work, email, content in a specific voice, formatted data, and any task where "I know it when I see it" is your standard. Instead of trying to articulate what makes your brand voice distinctive, you paste three examples of it.

The Structure of a Few-Shot Prompt

1
State the task briefly

One sentence describing what you need. Don't over-explain — the examples will do that work.

2
Provide 2–3 labeled examples

Use a consistent format: Input → Output, or Example 1 / Example 2. The examples are your real instructions.

3
Give the new input

Now ask for what you actually need, in the same format as your examples. Claude will extrapolate the pattern.

Rewrite product descriptions in our brand voice. Here are three examples:

Input: "Durable stainless steel water bottle, 32oz, keeps drinks cold."
Output: "Built for people who don't stop. 32oz of pure staying-cold-all-day capability — because lukewarm water at 2pm is not an option."

Input: "Noise-cancelling headphones with 30-hour battery life."
Output: "Thirty hours of the world going away. Put them on. Disappear into whatever matters."

Now rewrite this: "Lightweight running shoes with memory foam insole, available in 8 colors."

Advanced Application

Use negative examples too. Add "Here is an example of what NOT to do" with a brief note on why. Positive + negative examples together create sharper, more reliable outputs than positive examples alone.

Chapter 06
Output Control
Specify format, length, tone, and structure — and stop rewriting Claude's responses by hand
Chapter 06Output Control

Claude will default to a format it thinks is appropriate — but your idea of appropriate and Claude's idea may diverge significantly. The solution is simple: tell it exactly what you want. Output control instructions cost almost nothing to write and save significant editing time.

The Output Control Toolkit

CONTROL 01
Length
Word counts, paragraph counts, sentence limits. "In exactly 3 sentences." "Under 100 words." "At least 500 words but no more than 800."
CONTROL 02
Structure
Headers, bullets, numbered lists, prose only, table format, JSON, markdown. Be explicit: "No bullet points. Write in paragraphs only."
CONTROL 03
Tone & Register
Formal, casual, direct, warm, urgent, academic, funny, dry. You can stack these: "Warm but professional, direct without being blunt."
CONTROL 04
What to Exclude
Often as important as what to include. "No clichés." "Don't mention competitors." "Avoid jargon." "Don't start with 'Certainly!'."

Chapter 07
Iterative Refinement
How to sculpt a perfect response through targeted feedback and follow-up
Chapter 07Iterative Refinement

The first response is raw clay. Your job, as the prompt engineer, is to shape it. Professionals don't expect perfection on the first pass — they know that the best results come from a back-and-forth where each exchange gets more specific and more refined.

The key is giving feedback that is directional, not just evaluative. "Make it better" tells Claude nothing useful. "The second paragraph is too formal — rewrite it so it sounds like a conversation between friends" tells Claude exactly what to do.

Refinement Command Patterns

CommandWhat It Does
"Make this 30% shorter without losing the main points."Precision editing without word-by-word revision
"Rewrite the opening — it buries the lead."Targets a specific section for improvement
"What did you leave out that a skeptic would ask about?"Forces gap analysis
"Give me three alternative versions of the headline."Expands options before committing
"Now write a version targeting a senior executive audience."Re-frames existing content for a different reader
"Critique your own response. What's weak?"Self-evaluation that surfaces blind spots
The Sculptor's Approach

Great prompt engineers rarely start over. They build on what Claude has produced, narrowing in with each follow-up. Think of it as removing what doesn't belong, not starting from a blank page each time. Three rounds of specific refinement beats five new attempts at a perfect first prompt.

Advanced Techniques
Chaining prompts, meta-prompts, and building reusable systems that work every time
Chapter 08Advanced Techniques

1. Prompt Chaining

Instead of asking Claude to do everything in one enormous prompt, break complex tasks into a pipeline of focused steps. The output of step one becomes the input of step two. This dramatically improves quality on multi-part projects like research reports, content campaigns, or business analyses.

→
Step 1: Research & Extract

"Here is a 2,000-word article. Extract the 5 most important insights as bullet points."

→
Step 2: Analyze

"Based on these 5 insights, identify which ones are most relevant to a small business owner and explain why."

→
Step 3: Create

"Now write a 400-word LinkedIn article for small business owners based on the most relevant insights you identified."

2. The Meta-Prompt

Ask Claude to write the prompt for you. This is especially useful when you know what you want but struggle to articulate it precisely. Give Claude a high-level goal and ask it to produce a detailed prompt — then use or refine that prompt.

I need to write a prompt that will get Claude to analyze a startup's pitch deck and give actionable feedback as if from a Series A investor. Write me the ideal prompt for this task. Make it detailed, include role assignment, specific instructions for what to evaluate, and a clear output format.

3. System Instructions & Templates

For tasks you repeat regularly, invest once in writing a master template. A great reusable prompt pays for its creation cost within days. Store your best prompts in a personal library — Notion, a text file, wherever you can find them fast.

Build Your Prompt Library

For every task you run more than once, save the prompt that worked best. Over three months, you'll have a personal collection of tested, reliable prompts that outperform anything you'd generate fresh in the moment.

The Golden Rules

Be Specific
Vague prompts get vague answers. The more precise your request, the more targeted the response.
Give Context
Claude knows nothing about your situation unless you share it. Context is the most underused lever in prompting.
Iterate
First drafts are starting points. Three rounds of specific feedback beats one attempt at a perfect prompt.
Save What Works
Your best prompts are assets. Build a personal library and reuse prompts that have proven themselves.
